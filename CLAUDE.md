## Design Context

### Users
Job seekers — particularly those navigating a tough or uncertain labor market. They may be anxious, time-pressured, or unfamiliar with macroeconomic data. They come to this tool for a clear, trustworthy signal: *is now a good time to look for a job?* They are not economists; they need institutional data made legible and actionable. The tool is explicitly on their side.

### Brand Personality
**Trustworthy, clear, empowering.**

Voice: calm authority with warmth. Not corporate, not cold — more like a knowledgeable friend who happens to understand labor economics. The tool speaks plainly, avoids jargon, and always frames data in terms of what it means *for the user*.

Emotional goals: confidence, clarity, and a sense of agency. The user should leave feeling informed and capable, not overwhelmed.

### Aesthetic Direction
**Modern fintech / data journalism.** Think Bloomberg Terminal meets The Pudding — data-dense but beautifully edited. Editorial hierarchy, clean typography, purposeful use of color as signal (not decoration).

- Dark mode is the primary experience; light mode should feel equally considered
- JetBrains Mono for data/numbers/labels; Instrument Serif for editorial moments (headlines, key callouts)
- Color is strictly semantic: green = good for job seekers, red = bad, yellow/orange = neutral/caution
- Anti-references: avoid anything that feels like a generic SaaS dashboard, startup landing page, or government website

### Design Principles
1. **Data earns its presence.** Every element must justify its existence. No decorative chrome, no padding for its own sake.
2. **Color means something.** The green/yellow/orange/red scale is the visual language. Use it consistently; never use these hues decoratively.
3. **Hierarchy is editorial.** The score is the headline. Everything else supports or contextualizes it. Typography and sizing should make the reading order unmistakable.
4. **Accessibility is non-negotiable.** WCAG AA minimum throughout. Given the semantic color system, always pair color with a secondary signal (shape, text, icon).
5. **Pro-worker, not patronizing.** The interface should feel like it was built by someone who respects the user's intelligence and is genuinely on their side.
